What Is the Cocktail Party Problem, and What Does It Tells Us About Selective Attention?

The challenge of comprehending speech in a noisy environment has been called the cocktail party problem, on the analogy of trying to pay attention to one conversation among many simultaneous, overlapping conversations. This problem in selective attention has been studied in dichotic listening experiments, as illustrated in Figure 1. Participants wear headphones that transmit one spoken |message into one ear and a different spoken message into the other ear (e.g., two different mundane newspaper articles that don’t include any proper names or unusual words). As the messages play, participants must repeat back (or shadow) the message heard in one ear as the message unfolds, in order to ensure that they are perceiving and understanding that message. After shadowing one message for a minute or two, participants are asked what they heard in the other, ignored ear. Consistently, participants are able to report almost nothing about the content of the ignored message. In fact, even changing the language being spoken in the ignored ear, or playing the message in reverse, is often not noticed.

However, some changes tend to be noticed immediately—for example, when the unattended message is replaced by a tone or when a man’s voice is replaced by a woman’s. This suggests that low-level sensory changes in the ignored ear are noticed but that changes related to meaning aren’t. This conclusion, of course, raises a question: If meaning isn’t registered when you’re attending to one among many conversations, why do you notice your own name spoken in an unattended conversation at a party? Further studies have suggested that certain words (e.g., your own name or emotionally charged words such as sex and death) tend to capture your attention. This suggests that the semantic content of the ignored message is processed to some degree, but unless that message has particular importance for you, it’s discarded and unavailable for later recall.
